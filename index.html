Helping your toddler to talk

In this article :

How can I help my toddler learn to talk?
How can I make learning to talk fun?
When should I worry about my child's language development?


The more you chat to your toddler, the more opportunities she's likely to have to learn how to talk (DoH 2017).
But bear in mind that all children are different and learn to talk at different rates (Harding 2014).

How can I help my toddler learn to talk?

Talk as much and as often as you can to your toddler, when you're at home and when you're out and about. Research shows that the more you talk to your child, the better she'll become at using and understanding language (Weisleder et al 2013). When she talks back to you, try to give her your undivided attention if you can. It will encourage her to keep talking (DE 2010)! Focus on what your toddler is trying to say, rather than on how clearly she pronounces her words (DE 2010). Nodding, smiling and responding to her will help her feel more confident about talking to you.
When she uses words, give her plenty of praise. For example, you could tell her "Yes, that's right, it is a spoon – well done!" (DE 2010, NHS 2017) Let your toddler see what you mean, by matching what you do to what you say. "Shoes off" you could say, removing her shoes. Then "socks off", removing her socks. Get your toddler's attention by saying her name and making eye contact with her (DE 2010). This will help her to understand when you’re talking to her.
Give your toddler plenty of opportunities to talk during everyday activities. If you ask her a question, leave a good pause, so that she has time to answer you (NHS 2017). Introduce your toddler to situations where she can learn exciting new words. Take her on the bus or for a walk. Point out things you see when you're out and about (GOSH 2016, NHS 2017). You can also show her pictures in books and talk together about what you can see (GOSH 2016). Repeat back what you hear your toddler trying to say to you, even if she doesn't say it clearly. Expand on what she says. So if your toddler says "nana" when she wants a banana, you could say "Yes, here's a banana." (DE 2010, NHS 2017)
Simplify your speech. Use short sentences and emphasise key words when you are talking to your toddler. This will help her to focus on the important information. For example, rather than talking in a long sentence to say you're going out and she needs to put her shoes on, keep it short, such as, "We're going out now, shoes on please." (DE 2010) Use objects and gestures to help your little one understand the situation, as well as the words involved. For example, when you ask your little one, "Would you like an apple or an orange?" show her each of the fruits as you say the word, so that she realises the difference between them, and that there is a choice to be made.
Help your toddler to focus her attention on what you’re saying to her, by switching off unnecessary background noise such as the TV or radio (DE 2010, NHS 2017). Children can find it harder than adults to filter out background noise.

How can I make learning to talk fun?

Your toddler loves having fun with you. So if you make talking fun for your toddler, it will encourage her to use more words to express herself (DE 2010).
Get down on the floor and play with your toddler. Let her choose the toy or activity and talk about what she's doing (DE 2010). Join in with your toddler's play, repeat the words she’s using and make sure she can see your face and mouth when you talk. This will give her lots of opportunities to hear clear examples of the words she's learning (DE 2010, GOSH 2016).
Have fun pretending your toddler's favourite teddy or action figure is real. Include the toy in everyday activities. Sit teddy at the table for tea, for example, and put a bib on him. Talk about what teddy is doing while he does it.
Look at books with your toddler as part of your daily routine together. Even if you don't read the story word-for-word, she’ll learn by listening to you talking about the pictures (DE 2010, GOSH 2016, NHS 2017).
Have fun with nursery rhymes, especially those with actions such as "Row, row, row your boat". Verses, stories and songs with repetitive actions, rhythms and rhymes will help your toddler to remember the words (DE 2010, NHS 2017).
Play games where you have to take turns. Learning to wait for her turn will help your toddler to understand the natural back-and-forth of conversations (DE 2010, NHS 2017).
Try listening games with simple instructions such as:
Clapping games, where you clap a rhythm, and then wait for your toddler to try to clap the same rhythm.
Matching the noise of an animal to the right toy or picture. For example, you say "moo" and wait for your toddler to pick out a toy cow, or point to a picture of a cow. Gathering together several toys that make noises, and making a sound with one of them out of your toddler's sight. Ask your toddler to show you which toy made the noise. (DE 2010)
You could also try a game of "Simon says". Call out simple commands like "Simon says, put your hands on your head" and demonstrate the command yourself. Then let your toddler do the same. Carry on with other commands, such as "Simon says put your hands on your knees", or "Simon says cross your arms."
When the weather's warm, take a big towel or blanket into the garden or local park and lie down on it with your toddler. Ask her to close her eyes and listen carefully. After a minute or so ask her what she could hear. You can then talk about the wind in the trees, birdsong, or a dog barking.


When should I worry about my child's language development?

All children are different, and some develop faster than others. However, it's easy to worry if your child seems slower to talk or to understand what you say, compared to her sibling or peers. Sometimes, boys can be slower to talk than girls, or toddlers can talk later if their parents also did (Harding 2014).
Ask your health visitor or GP for advice, if:

by one year of age your child isn't babbling double syllables, such as 'da da' or 'ma ma' by 18 months old, she's using fewer than six words, or showing no signs of understanding what you’re saying.
by two and a half years old, she's unable to speak in two or three word sentences (Harding 2014)
If necessary, your GP or health visitor will refer your child to a speech and language therapist (NHS 2017). Sometimes a hearing test may be recommended too, as undetected hearing problems can be linked to difficulties learning to talk (DE 2010).
If your toddler does have a problem speaking, then the sooner you seek help, the more likely it is that she'll catch up with her peers (Harding 2014). In many cases, all that's needed is a little extra support or reassurance from your health visitor (Harding 2014).

ABC of potty training

In this article : Assess your child's readiness for potty training Potty training accessories to help your child Be consistent in your approach to potty training Cope calmly with potty-training accidents Potty training is one milestone that you're probably eagerly awaiting. Though some toddlers adjust in a few days, bear in mind that it may take your little one several months to get into the potty habit. And there will be setbacks along the way. Assess your child's readiness for potty training There's no right age to start potty training, as every child is different. Parents usually think about training when their child is between 18 months and three years old. Most children are between two years and two-and-a-half years when they start. Don't feel pushed into toilet training your child too soon due to pressure from other parents or your family. Watch for the signs that your child is ready to start training, and don't start before then. Talk to your toddler about what you are going to do, and decide what you're going to call wee and poo! Make potty-training preparations Go on a special trip with your child to buy some new pants and a potty. A potty is probably easiest to start with, rather than a toilet. It's easy to get on and off, and can be moved around the house. However, you may want to buy a training seat to attach to your toilet. If your child uses a toilet seat, you'll also need a footstep so your child can stabilise himself with his feet and push when he's having a poo. It will also allow him to get on and off the loo independently. You could show your toddler a fun picture book or DVD about using the potty, to get him interested in what's to come. You could try using training pants for your toddler instead of, or as well as, proper underwear. Cloth training pants are similar to regular pants, but have an absorbent pad inside to cope with small accidents. Absorbent, disposable pull-up trainer pants are especially easy to pull up and down. Some parents find these convenient, but some don't find them helpful, as they don't feel very different from a nappy. Wearing real underwear may encourage your toddler to use his potty. You could let him choose some pants which have his favourite cartoon character on them. Potty training accessories to help your child There are lots of products out there that can help you and your child with potty training. From the potty itself to sticker charts to praise their progress, there’s plenty of things that could make the potty training journey a little bit easier.
